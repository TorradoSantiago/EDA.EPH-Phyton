Why this dataset matters

The Annual Household Survey is valuable because it captures demographic, educational, labor, and health conditions across all communes of Buenos Aires. In the original notebook, that context was central: the dataset was presented as a policy tool, not just as material for plotting charts. That framing is preserved here because it makes the analysis more credible.

Executive takeaway: the project is not just about showing inequality. It is about using a citywide survey to understand which social and labor-market variables are most strongly associated with income differences.
  • Does geography influence household income?
  • How does household size affect total and per-capita income?
  • What is the relationship between schooling and labor income?
  • How much of the gender income gap can be explained by observed characteristics?

What the descriptive and econometric evidence says

The current version keeps the original hypothesis-driven structure but improves the measurement. It moves from descriptive comparisons into a Mincer earnings equation and an Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition.

What the ML layer adds

The original presentation was strong because it was hypothesis-first. The ML layer is therefore treated here as a robustness test, not as the main attraction. I benchmarked four out-of-sample models on log labor income to see whether more flexibility materially improved fit.

Why this matters for hiring

This repository works well in interviews because it shows range without becoming noisy. It starts with a real public dataset, moves into an interpretable labor-economics model, and then tests whether more flexible ML materially changes the result. Just as important, it preserves the original academic rationale of the first delivery.

Hiring takeaway: this is the kind of project that signals good judgment. It combines theory, policy relevance, econometrics, and ML benchmarking without confusing prediction with explanation.
