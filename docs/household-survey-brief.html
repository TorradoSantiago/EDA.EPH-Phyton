Purpose and hypothesis

The project asks a simple but important question: how are income differences distributed across Buenos Aires when we read geography, household size, and education together?

Main hypothesis: the strongest income differences are not random. They are structured by territory and reinforced by household composition.
  • Higher-income communes should also show stronger per-capita income values.
  • Larger households should face more pressure on income per person.
  • Education should still matter, but not enough to explain the whole socioeconomic picture on its own.

What the evidence shows

The new script makes the project reproducible outside the notebook. That matters because it converts exploratory work into exportable tables and figures.

Improved conclusion

The project is strongest when it avoids a superficial “rich versus poor communes” story. The more credible conclusion is that territorial inequality becomes sharper when household size is considered alongside household income.

  • Commune 14 combines high household income with a relatively small average household size of 2.15 members.
  • Commune 8 combines lower household income with a much larger average household size of 3.21 members.
  • Per-capita income therefore diverges more sharply than household income alone suggests: 37,000 versus roughly 12,500.
Professional takeaway: this repository now reads like a compact urban inequality diagnostic, not just a classroom notebook.

That makes it more useful for analytics, policy, and consulting conversations, especially when the audience wants a clear interpretation rather than code-first exploration.
