# Executive Brief

## In one sentence

Applied labor-economics case on Buenos Aires household-survey microdata, combining territorial diagnostics with a Mincer earnings equation and an Oaxaca-Blinder gender-gap decomposition.

## What problem it addresses

The project asks two linked questions: where do income differences appear across the city, and how much of the labor-income gap between men and women can be explained by observable characteristics such as schooling, experience, commune, and occupational category?

## What a non-technical reader can see

- A relevant public dataset used to study inequality in Buenos Aires.
- Clear descriptive outputs on communes, education, and household structure.
- A formal econometric layer that upgrades the case beyond charts.
- A concrete insight: the observed gender income gap is not explained by weaker measured characteristics among women in the sample.

## What it demonstrates

- Comfort with public microdata and socioeconomic questions.
- The ability to move from exploratory analysis to econometric modeling.
- Strong fit for consulting, policy analytics, labor economics, and strategy roles.

## Current headline findings

- Commune `14` has roughly double the median household income of Commune `8`.
- Each additional year of schooling is associated with a `13.25%` labor-income premium in the working sample.
- The male-female labor-income gap is `29.35%`, with a large unexplained component in the Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition.
