# Executive Brief

## In one sentence

Applied urban and labor-market case on Buenos Aires survey microdata, combining territorial diagnostics, an interpretable earnings model, and a compact ML benchmark.

## What problem it addresses

The project asks where income differences appear across the city, how much schooling seems to matter for labor income, and whether more complex ML models add substantially more explanatory power than an interpretable baseline.

## What a non-technical reader can see

- A relevant public dataset used to study inequality in Buenos Aires.
- Clear descriptive outputs on communes, education, and household structure.
- A formal econometric layer that quantifies the schooling premium and the gender gap.
- A practical ML layer that tests whether more complex models really improve prediction.

## What it demonstrates

- Comfort with public microdata and socially relevant questions.
- Ability to move from exploratory analysis to econometric reasoning.
- Judgment about when ML helps and when interpretation matters more.
- Strong fit for consulting, policy analytics, labor economics, and strategy roles.

## Current headline findings

- Commune `14` has roughly double the median household income of Commune `8`.
- Each additional year of schooling is associated with a `13.25%` labor-income premium.
- The male-female labor-income gap is `29.35%`, with a large unexplained component.
- The best ML benchmark reaches `R^2 = 0.342`, which only modestly improves on the linear benchmark.
